Surreal Photomontage with Everyday Objects Workshop

Lockdown has prevented us from leaving our homes, a chance to become better acquainted with our everyday objects! This online workshop will teach you how to make your own original photomontages featuring own photos, found imagery and the everyday objects in your home.

Theadora is an artist and tutor specialising in photomontage and video. She transforms hand tools and objects from archival material and photographs from her travels into surreal artworks, this workshop will encourage you to scroll through your smartphone gallery with intent, selecting own photos as backdrops and foregrounds and throwing your objects into the bizarre of the surreal sphere.

This is a free open submission process welcoming photomontages both internationally and nationally. The prize is your photomontage translated onto a monochrome A5 photoetching plate and an edition of 5x prints sent to the artist on somerset paper to sign, title and keep.

Entries to be sent via email: theobway@gmail.com by the 15th June to be in with a chance of winning.

 

Materials:

Begin by collecting photographical imagery from newspaper clippings, books, magazines or your own photographs. I love to look through my own photo gallery on my phone and discover old places and incidences that I chose to record and may not have used. It's helpful to seek interesting backgrounds or situations that could heighten your object's appeal. You will need some images of your objects or Google is a great tool to look for images or royalty free websites like 'PxHere', if collecting from the internet ensure you go to: tools - size and try to select larger images to secure good printable quality.

 

Digital programmes are a great way to photomontage, I use Adobe Photoshop but Gimp, Photoshop Essentials and 'paint.net' is a free app and are also very strong montaging tools. Thick paper or card is useful to stick your subjects down if not using a digital app. Sewing scissors are a great way to cut out fine details and a little masking tape to hold your cuttings into place.

 

 

Step 1 - Collect your Montage Material

 

Let's begin by selecting your object, which could be anything significant to you or perhaps it was significant of your lockdown experience or are you on the brink of throwing it away and this is your artistic good bye, maybe it is considered disregarded and you'd like to give it more credit or authority. You could repeat and multiply forms of it or just have it as itself. Think about the setting and how the object could fit within its environment. What possibilities could ensue? Look for different background options and see where your object makes the best fit, remember you could always photograph images and re-print them to get the correct scale. They could end up at the park, your living room, the jungle, in space or in a historical photograph. Scrolling through your smartphone gallery will help you to get personal, contemporary images of the here and now or visit a favourite lockdown location and photograph it. Be sure to collect and shoot any imagery that could work.

 

 

 

Step 2 - Shoot your object

 

To photograph the object I usually take many photos, often my objects are stainless steel and reflective. To avoid reflections I take them outside and stand on highground to get a seamless photograph. To shoot at home I often set up a photography studio out of white foamboard and paper. Using a tripod or a stool will enable you to get a steady hand with your camera and avoid unfocused imagery. Take lots of photographs of the object in different positions and angles to gain multiple perspectives. The more images, the more to choose from. Think about what viewpoint you would like to achieve, worms eye view can make your object look super human and larger than it is.

 

 

 

Step 3 - Cut and Stick

 

Next, sit your object in its environment and begin to arrange them on several backgrounds thinking of meaning and expression to achieve results. Practice moving them around different skies or even textures - using archival material can also make an interesting setting. Think about how your objects best fit within this new created setting. You could add extra detail also to add to the composition, extra smaller subjects for instance. Use cutting tools from scissors to the quick selection tool (if digital) to remove the background from your object. Use a little masking tape or blu tack to hold them in place. I often take photographs of my new compositions or if digital, screenshots, so I have several photo-sketches and I can select my preferred outcome to work on.
