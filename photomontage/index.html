Surreal Photomontage with Everyday Objects Workshop
by Theadora Ballantyne-Way


Lockdown has prevented us from leaving our homes, a chance to become better acquainted with our everyday objects! This online workshop will teach you how to make original photomontages featuring your own photos, found imagery and the everyday objects in your home. One selected winner will have their photomontage editioned into a photo etched print!

Theadora is an artist and tutor specialising in photomontage and video. She transforms hand tools and objects from archival material and photographs from her travels into surreal artworks, this workshop will encourage you to scroll through your smartphone gallery with intent, selecting own photos as backdrops and foregrounds and throwing your objects into the surreal sphere.

When you have completed this workshop please send in your photomontages and I will select a prizewinner! The prize is, your photomontage to be translated onto an A5 photoetching plate and a monochrome edition of 10x prints sent to the artist on Somerset paper to sign, title and keep.

Entries to be sent via email to: theobway@gmail.com by the 3rd August to be in with a chance of winning.

Step 1. Collect your Photomontage Materials

Photomontage Tools

To begin, decide if you would rather make an analogue or digital photomontage. If digital there are a range of software choices perfect for photomontaging. I usually use Adobe Photoshop but pixlr.com, Gimp, Pixelmator, Paint.net or Photoshop-Elements are also great tools and available free or with a free trial. If analogue you will need a ruler for tearing large parts from mags, masking tape to tac and hold pieces, nail/sewing scissors are great for fine detail and tweezers to help pick up delicate photomontage parts.

Photomontage Materials

  
Collect photographical imagery from newspaper clippings, books, magazines, I often find postcards to be a great resource too but particularly your own photographs. I love to look through my own photo gallery on my phone and discover old places and incidences that I chose to record and may not have used yet. It’s helpful to seek interesting backgrounds or situations that could heighten your object’s appeal. Scrolling through your smartphone gallery will help you to get personal, contemporary set of images of the here and now to work with or visit a favourite lockdown exercise spot and photograph it. Be sure to collect and shoot a range of different possibilities so you have lots of options for your object. I have included a variety of printable images that you can download to help you to construct your artwork or Google is a great tool to look for images or royalty free websites like PxHere and Unsplash, if collecting images from the Google ensure you go to: ‘tools’ then ‘size’ and select ‘large’ to secure good printable quality.

Select Your Object

Start by selecting your object, which could be anything overlooked or perhaps significant of your lockdown experience or maybe it is just a great object! Think about the setting based on your collected materials and how the object could fit within its new environment. What possibilities could occur? Choose a range of different background options from your material and see where your object makes the best fit, remember you could always photograph images and re-print/size them to get the correct scale. They could end up strolling through the park, a new feature in your living room, the sorcerer of a new dystopia, misplaced in the jungle, in space or proven to be troublesome in a historical photograph etc...

Step 2.  Record and Shoot your Object

  
You will need some images of your objects, photographing your own is best. To photograph my object I usually take many photos, often the objects I use are stainless steel and reflective. To avoid reflections take them outside and stand on high ground to create a seamless photograph. To shoot at home, I often set up a photography studio out of white foamboard and paper. Using a tripod or a stool will enable you to get a steady hand with your camera and avoid unfocused imagery. Take lots of photographs of the object in different positions and angles to gain multiple perspectives. The more images, the more to choose from. Think about what viewpoint you would like to achieve, worms eye view can make your object look super human and larger than it is, and birds eye gives an aerial perspective for example.

Step 3.  Trim and Cut your Object

  
Next, remove the background of your object. I usually use Photoshop and the quick selection tool or zoom in and erase for precision or nail/sewing scissors for detail if you aren’t using software. With your cut-out object the fun can really start!

Step 4.  Place and Review

  
Experiment by placing your object into multiple backgrounds with the images you have collected. Where you place it can completely change their meaning and original intention, this is your chance to release their potential! You could enhance your composition with foreground features or build layers surrounding your object, adding extra detail by incorporating other components to add to your object’s setting. It’s a great idea to save or photograph different variants of your compositions as this will give you an idea of which outcome you want to take forward and complete to stick.

Step 5.  Assemblage and Final Details

Once totally decided on an arrangement, it can often help to re-shoot an object taking into account angles, scale, lighting so it fits perfectly within the composition. If this is appropriate to you, you could re-shoot your object to the correct angle to fit in your composition to print again or re-scale it in your software. For best quality outcome I find going back a step ensures a pristine finish.

When complete be sure to stick all layers down carefully or export your digital composition. Uploading your outcome into photo enhancing software, such as Photoshop or Google Photos (free) will allow you to crop your image and I find to have some great edit sliders for lighting and colour options. Turn up the blacks and tones to create mystery and highlight areas of interest on your composition.
